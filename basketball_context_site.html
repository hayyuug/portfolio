Chapter 01 · Origins

The Roots Run Deeper Than Basketball

Long before anyone in the NBA had heard the word "Moreyball," the seeds of the analytics revolution were being planted in an entirely different arena — World War II.

Operations researchers — scientists who had applied statistical methods to military logistics and strategy during the war — began turning those same tools toward athletic competition in the 1950s. According to the Smithsonian Institution, the sports analytics community grew from three overlapping groups: operations researchers, freelance sports journalists, and internet hobbyists.

"The tension between data and tradition in sports is not a recent invention — it is a conflict that intensified slowly across generations before exploding into public view."

This history matters. The conflict you see on the court today was not created by any single executive or algorithm. It was fifty years in the making.

Chapter 04 · The Clash

When the Spreadsheet Meets the Soul

As data reshaped NBA front offices, it produced a visible and growing tension on the floor. Players who had spent their entire lives mastering skills that analytics now labeled "inefficient" found themselves caught between their training and their employer's spreadsheets.

The cultural clash reflects something deeper than a dispute about shot selection. It is a debate about whether the soul of the game belongs to the people who play it — or the people who measure it.

Former NBA star Charles Barkley called analytics advocates "idiots who believe in analytics" on live television — blunt language, but reflecting a genuine generational divide between people who learned basketball through feel and those who learned it through formulas.

The Takeaway

The Stakes Are Real. The Roots Run Deep.

The modern NBA — with its three-point barrages and spreadsheet-driven offenses — is the direct product of fifty years of statistical thinking, one watershed moment in baseball, and one general manager's willingness to rebuild an entire offense around math. The question of what basketball is supposed to be is far from settled.
